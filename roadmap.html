StegVerse Public Roadmap

Green-light roadmap for formalisms, tests, papers, and proof surfaces.

This page tracks when each StegVerse advancement is ready to move from idea, to formalism, to executable test, to paper, to public proof surface.

Roadmap rule: an advancement is green-lit only when its formal definition, test harness, evidence output, and publication path are visible enough to be independently inspected.

Green-light sequence

No silent promotion
1

Define the formalism

The concept receives a canonical repo, minimal definitions, variables, admissibility conditions, and a clear boundary statement.

Green light: the formalism can be read without relying on private conversation context.
2

Build the test surface

The corresponding test repo receives deterministic examples, failure cases, and executable checks.

Green light: the test repo can produce ALLOW, DENY, or FAIL_CLOSED from stated inputs.
3

Generate receipts and replay evidence

Each test emits evidence showing the proposed transition, decision basis, outcome, and replay path.

Green light: an external reader can reconstruct why the transition was accepted or rejected.
4

Promote to paper track

Validated formalism/test pairs become paper sections, theorem candidates, figures, and claims.

Green light: every major paper claim points to a formal definition or executable proof surface.
5

Publish the proof surface

The public page links the formalism repo, test repo, paper draft, demo artifact, and receipt outputs.

Green light: StegVerse can show admissibility before effect, not just explanation after effect.

Done criteria

Verification required
  • ✓Canonical formalism repo exists.
  • ✓Matching formalism-test repo exists.
  • ✓Minimal executable test produces deterministic outcomes.
  • ✓Receipts or reports explain the decision basis.
  • ✓Paper draft references the formalism and test evidence.
  • ✓Public proof surface links all relevant artifacts.

Public category anchor

Use consistently

StegVerse is commit-time admissibility infrastructure for consequence-bearing systems.

It does not merely record what happened after consequence binds. It determines whether a proposed transition has sufficient admissibility to become effect-capable at all.

Execution rule: no admissibility at commit, no mutation.
